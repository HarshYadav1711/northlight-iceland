Northern Lights

Aurora viewing depends on clear skies, darkness, and solar activity. This guide covers what you can control — and what you cannot.

Understanding aurora

What causes the lights

The aurora borealis appears when charged particles from the sun interact with gases in Earth's atmosphere. Green is the most common colour; reds and purples appear during stronger activity.

No forecast guarantees a show. Treat aurora hunting as one part of a wider winter trip — Iceland offers plenty to see even on cloudy nights.

Timing

When to look

Season

Dark skies return from late August through mid-April. Peak viewing runs from October to March, when nights are longest.

Time of night

Activity often picks up between 22:00 and 02:00, but displays can appear anytime after sunset. Check local sunset times — they shift quickly in spring and autumn.

Weather

Cloud cover is the main obstacle. Use the Icelandic Met Office aurora forecast alongside standard weather apps. Be ready to drive to clearer patches.

Light pollution

Leave Reykjavík for darker skies. Þingvellir, the Reykjanes Peninsula, and rural guesthouses north of the capital are reliable starting points.

Preparation

What to wear and bring

You may stand outside for an hour or more. Layering matters more than any single heavy coat.

  • Base layers: Merino or synthetic — avoid cotton against skin.
  • Insulation: Fleece or down mid-layer; windproof outer shell.
  • Extremities: Insulated boots, two pairs of gloves (liner plus waterproof), hat that covers ears.
  • Equipment: Headlamp with red-light mode, tripod for photography, hand warmers.
  • Safety: Tell someone your route. Park only in designated areas and watch for ice on paths.

Realistic expectations

A show is never guaranteed

Commercial tours can improve your odds by chasing clearer skies, but they cannot control solar activity. Budget for two or three evening attempts if aurora is a priority.

Cameras capture more colour than the naked eye during weak displays. A faint grey-green arc can look dramatic in a long exposure — that is normal, not a disappointment.

For current conditions, consult the Icelandic Met Office aurora forecast.

Planning a winter visit?

Combine aurora evenings with daylight activities — ice caves, geothermal pools, and coastal walks still fill a full itinerary.
