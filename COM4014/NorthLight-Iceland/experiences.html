Experiences

Activities that reward planning — what to book ahead, what works in each season, and what you can do independently.

Iceland's landscape is the main attraction. These experiences add structure to your days without filling every hour with organised tours.

Geothermal pools

From the Blue Lagoon to local swimming pools in every town — hot water is part of daily life. Shower before entering; it is required by pool etiquette.

Glacier walks

Guided walks on Sólheimajökull or Vatnajökull outlets run year-round. Never walk on a glacier without equipment and a certified guide — crevasses are invisible from the surface.

Whale watching

Húsavík in the north and Reykjavík harbour both run regular trips. Summer offers the widest species variety; dress warmly on boats even in August.

Culture

Museums and heritage

Reykjavík's National Museum of Iceland and the Settlement Exhibition trace Viking history in compact, well-designed galleries. Perlan's Wonders of Iceland exhibition suits families and bad-weather days.

In the north, the Goðafoss waterfall carries historical weight — it is where lawspeaker Þorgeir converted Iceland to Christianity around the year 1000. Combine the visit with Lake Mývatn's volcanic formations.

Self-guided

Free and low-cost options

Public swimming pools

Every town has a heated pool — typically 900–1,200 ISK entry. Hot tubs and steam baths included. A genuine local experience.

Waterfall walks

Seljalandsfoss, Skógafoss, and Gullfoss are accessible from parking areas. Paths can be icy in winter — microspikes help.

Coastal walks

Arnarstapi to Hellnar on Snæfellsnes and the Reykjanes coastal paths need no guide. Respect cliff edges and warning signs.
