Experiences

Five activities travellers ask about most — when they run, what they cost in time and effort, and how to approach each one safely.

Experience listings

Winter

Northern Lights

Aurora appears when skies are dark and clear — roughly late August through mid-April, with peak darkness from October to March. Leave city light pollution for Þingvellir, the Reykjanes Peninsula, or rural guesthouses.

No forecast guarantees a display. Plan two or three evening attempts if aurora is a priority. Dress for standing still in cold wind for an hour or more.

Icelandic Met Office aurora forecast

Year-round

Glacier hiking

Guided walks on Sólheimajökull and Vatnajökull outlets run throughout the year. Crampons, harnesses, and ropes are provided — you need reasonable fitness and warm layers.

Never walk on a glacier without a certified guide. Crevasses are hidden beneath surface snow and ice.

Year-round

Hot springs & geothermal pools

The Blue Lagoon near Keflavík is the best-known option — book well ahead in summer. Every town has a public swimming pool (sundlaug) with hot tubs and steam baths for a fraction of the price.

Shower thoroughly before entering any pool. It is required etiquette and keeps the water clean for everyone.

Summer peak

Whale watching

Húsavík in the north and Reykjavík harbour both run regular departures. Humpbacks, minke whales, and dolphins are common from May through September. Winter trips are limited and weather-dependent.

Allow a backup day in case seas are too rough. Motion sickness tablets help on Atlantic swells.

Nov–Mar

Ice caves

Natural blue ice caves form at Vatnajökull glacier outlets during winter. Access is guided only — caves collapse and reform each year, so routes change season to season.

Combine with a South Coast overnight in Höfn or Vik. Day trips from Reykjavík are possible but involve long drives.
