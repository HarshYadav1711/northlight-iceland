Travel Guide

Practical information for planning an Iceland trip — seasons, getting around, costs, safety, and what to pack.

Timing

Best time to visit

There is no single best month — it depends on whether you want long hiking days, aurora nights, or fewer crowds.

Summer (June–August)

Midnight sun, highland access, and puffins. Peak prices and busiest roads. Book accommodation early.

Shoulder (May, September)

Moderate crowds and prices. September adds early aurora potential. Weather changes fast — pack layers.

Winter (November–March)

Short days, aurora focus, ice caves. Some roads close. A 4×4 helps in remote areas.

Spring (April–May)

Longer days return. Highlands still closed. Good balance of value and daylight for ring-road trips.

Getting around

Transport

Rental car: The most flexible option. Route 1 (Ring Road) is paved year-round. Gravel roads and single-lane bridges are common. F-roads into the highlands require a 4×4 and open only in summer.

Domestic flights: Air Iceland Connect links Reykjavík to Akureyri, Egilsstaðir, and Ísafjörður — useful for skipping long drives.

Bus tours: Straeto runs limited intercity routes. Day tours from Reykjavík cover the Golden Circle and South Coast without a car.

Check road.is and vedur.is every morning before long drives.

Costs

Budget

Iceland is expensive by most standards. Self-catering and shoulder-season travel reduce costs significantly.

Typical daily costs per person (ISK, approximate)
Category Budget Mid-range Comfort
Accommodation 5,000–8,000 15,000–25,000 30,000+
Food 3,000–5,000 8,000–12,000 15,000+
Transport Buses / shared tours Compact car + fuel 4×4 + fuel
Activities Free hikes, public pools One guided tour Multiple tours

Supermarkets Bónus and Krónan offer the best grocery prices. Tap water is safe and free — bring a reusable bottle.

Stay safe

Safety

Weather

Conditions change within hours. Wind can push cars on open roads. Never ignore road closure signs.

Coast & cliffs

Sneaker waves at black-sand beaches have caused fatalities. Stay behind marked zones at Reynisfjara and Djúpalónssandur.

Glaciers & ice

Never walk on ice without a guide. Thin crust over water and hidden crevasses are deadly.

What to bring

Packing

All seasons

  • Waterproof jacket and trousers
  • Sturdy walking boots
  • Layering system (base, mid, shell)
  • Sunglasses — low sun and snow glare
  • EU Type F plug adapter (230 V)

Winter additions

  • Thermal base layers and insulated gloves
  • Ice grips for boots (optional)
  • Headlamp for aurora viewing
  • Hand warmers for long outdoor waits
