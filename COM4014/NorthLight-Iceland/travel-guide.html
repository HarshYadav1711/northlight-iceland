Travel Guide

Practical information for planning an Iceland trip — seasons, getting around, costs, safety, and what to pack.

Timing

Best time to visit

There is no single best month — it depends on whether you want long hiking days, aurora nights, or fewer crowds.

Summer (June–August)

Midnight sun, highland access, and puffins along the coast. Peak prices and busiest roads. Book accommodation early, especially around the South Coast and Golden Circle.

Shoulder (May, September)

Moderate crowds and prices. September adds early aurora potential and autumn colour. Weather changes fast — pack layers and check forecasts daily.

Winter (November–March)

Short days, aurora focus, and ice cave access. Some roads close without warning. A 4×4 helps in remote areas; studded tyres are standard on rental cars.

Spring (April–May)

Longer days return and prices dip before summer. Highlands remain closed until June. Good balance of value and daylight for Ring Road trips.

Getting around

Transport

Rental car: The most flexible option for most itineraries. Route 1 (Ring Road) is paved year-round. Gravel roads and single-lane bridges are common outside the capital region. F-roads into the highlands require a 4×4 and open only in summer.

Domestic flights: Air Iceland Connect links Reykjavík to Akureyri, Egilsstaðir, and Ísafjörður — useful for skipping long drives to the north or Westfjords.

Bus tours: Straeto runs limited intercity routes. Day tours from Reykjavík cover the Golden Circle and South Coast without a car.

Check road.is and vedur.is every morning before long drives.

Costs

Budget

Iceland is expensive by most standards. Self-catering and shoulder-season travel reduce costs significantly.

Typical daily costs per person (ISK, approximate)
Category Budget Mid-range Comfort
Accommodation 5,000–8,000 15,000–25,000 30,000+
Food 3,000–5,000 8,000–12,000 15,000+
Transport Buses / shared tours Compact car + fuel 4×4 + fuel
Activities Free hikes, public pools One guided tour Multiple tours

Supermarkets Bónus and Krónan offer the best grocery prices. Tap water is safe and free — bring a reusable bottle. Alcohol is sold only in state-run Vínbúðin shops with limited hours.

Stay safe

Safety

Iceland is low-crime but high-risk for weather and terrain. Respect closures and local advice — conditions change within hours.

Weather & roads

Wind can push cars on open roads and close bridges without notice. Never ignore road closure signs or drive into water crossing a flooded section.

Coast & cliffs

Sneaker waves at black-sand beaches have caused fatalities. Stay behind marked zones at Reynisfjara and Djúpalónssandur — never turn your back on the ocean.

Glaciers & ice

Never walk on ice without a certified guide. Thin crust over water and hidden crevasses are deadly, even close to parking areas.

Before you go out

Register your travel plan at SafeTravel.is, download the 112 Iceland app, and tell someone your route when heading into remote areas.

What to bring

Packing

Layers matter more than any single heavy coat. Wind and rain are the constants — regardless of season.

All seasons

  • Waterproof jacket and trousers
  • Sturdy walking boots with ankle support
  • Layering system (base, mid, shell)
  • Sunglasses — low sun and snow glare year-round
  • EU Type F plug adapter (230 V)

Winter additions

  • Thermal base layers and insulated gloves
  • Ice grips for boots (optional but useful)
  • Headlamp for aurora viewing and short daylight
  • Hand warmers for long outdoor waits
