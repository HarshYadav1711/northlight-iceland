Destinations

Five regions that shape most Iceland itineraries. Each section covers drive time, ideal stay length, and what makes the route worth including.

Capital

Reykjavík

Most flights land at Keflavík, roughly 45 minutes from the city centre. Reykjavík works as a base for day trips and a place to recover from jet lag before heading out.

Hallgrímskirkja, Harpa, and the old harbour fill a comfortable day. Restaurants book up on weekends — reserve ahead if you have a short list in mind.

  • Drive from KEF: 45 minutes
  • Ideal stay: 2–3 nights
  • Best season: Year-round
Day route

Golden Circle

Þingvellir, Geysir, and Gullfoss form the classic loop — about 230 km and manageable in a single day from Reykjavík. Þingvellir sits on the rift between tectonic plates; Geysir erupts every few minutes; Gullfoss drops in two stages into a canyon.

Arrive before 10:00 in summer to avoid the busiest parking. Check road.is before winter drives.

Coast & waterfalls

South Coast

Route 1 south passes Seljalandsfoss and Skógafoss within two hours. Continue to Reynisfjara black-sand beach and Vík for a strong two-day route with an overnight stop.

Coastal winds are fierce. Stay behind marked paths at beaches — sneaker waves are dangerous. Paths can be icy from November through March.

North hub

Akureyri

Iceland's second city sits at the head of Eyjafjörður fjord. It is compact, walkable, and a practical base for Lake Mývatn, Goðafoss, and whale watching from Húsavík.

Domestic flights from Reykjavík take about 45 minutes if you prefer not to drive the full ring road. Allow four days minimum if driving from the capital.

Remote west

Westfjords

The least visited corner of Iceland rewards slow travel. Dynjandi waterfall, Látrabjarg bird cliffs, and fishing villages like Ísafjörður feel far from the ring-road crowds.

Gravel roads and long distances mean this region suits a dedicated trip rather than a quick detour. Summer is the practical window for most routes.
